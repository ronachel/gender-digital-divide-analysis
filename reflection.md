# Reflection: Beyond Data

## Rethinking the Gender Digital Divide

While this project analyzes global disparities in internet access using quantitative data, it also raises important questions about the limits of data in representing lived realities.

Metrics such as internet access rates and usage statistics provide valuable insights into patterns of inequality. However, they do not fully capture the social, cultural, and structural conditions that shape how individuals experience and engage with digital technologies.

For example, access to the internet does not necessarily translate to meaningful participation. Factors such as digital literacy, economic constraints, gender norms, and cultural expectations influence how individuals use technology in their everyday lives. These dimensions are often difficult to quantify, yet they play a critical role in shaping outcomes reflected in the data.

This highlights a broader issue in knowledge production: while data is often treated as objective and authoritative, it can overlook context and lived experience. In the case of the gender digital divide, statistical indicators may obscure the everyday realities of women navigating access, agency, and participation in digital spaces.

Reflecting on this, the project emphasizes the importance of integrating participatory and qualitative approaches alongside data analysis. Engaging directly with communities and incorporating their perspectives can provide a more nuanced and inclusive understanding of digital inequality.

Ultimately, this project is not only an analysis of disparities but also a reflection on how knowledge is constructed—what is measured, what is prioritized, and what remains unseen. It reinforces the need for more inclusive approaches to knowledge production that recognize the value of lived experience alongside data.
